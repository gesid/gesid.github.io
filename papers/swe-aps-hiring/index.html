Abstract

[Context] The escalating demand for Software Engineering (SE) professionals emphasizes the criticality of effective online Recruitment and Selection (R&S) processes. However, these processes often present particular challenges for novice software engineers, whose perspectives on problematic R&S practices remain largely underexplored.

[Objective] This paper aims to identify and characterize recurring Anti-patterns (defined as practices perceived by candidates as ineffective, counterproductive, or unfair) within online R&S processes, specifically from the viewpoint of early-career software engineers.

[Method] Employing an exploratory qualitative approach grounded in Applicant Attribution-Reaction Theory (AART), we conducted semi-structured online interviews with 22 novice software engineers in Brazil. Their combined experience encompasses over 470 online R&S processes. We used thematic analysis to examine their R&S experiences.

[Results] Our analysis revealed 10 central themes that shape candidate perceptions and led to the identification of 25 distinct Anti-patterns (APs). These APs span various facets of the R&S journey, including a lack of clarity in job advertisements, inadequate or absent feedback, poorly conducted process stages, misaligned technical and behavioral assessments, and overarching concerns about fairness and transparency.

[Conclusion] This study provides empirically grounded findings into the APs encountered by novice SE professionals during online R&S. By characterizing these detrimental practices, we also offer a set of takeaways for organizations aiming to refine their R&S strategies, enhance candidate experience, and more effectively attract early-career talent.
