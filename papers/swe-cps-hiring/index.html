Abstract

[Context] For many early-career software engineers, online Recruitment and Selection (R&S) processes are the main entry point into the profession, yet these experiences often fail to align with candidates' expectations. While research has documented recurring flaws in tech hiring, less attention has been given to the practices that systematically foster positive candidate experiences.

[Objective] This paper aims to identify and characterize these positive practices, which we term "Constructive Patterns" (CPs), from the perspective of novice software engineers.

[Method] Guided by Applicant Attribution-Reaction Theory (AART), we conducted 22 semi-structured interviews with early-career developers who collectively reported over 470 online R&S experiences.

[Results] Our thematic analysis identified 22 CPs, including, for example, Comprehensive and Transparent Job Advertisements (CP01), Specific and Developmental Feedback (CP03), Humanized and Respectful Interaction (CP06), and Framing the Process as a Two-Way Street (CP18).

[Conclusion] These findings contribute to advance the discussion from diagnosing dysfunctions to designing tech human-centered hiring, offering an empirically grounded catalog of CPs that organizations can use to better attract and support early-career software engineers.
